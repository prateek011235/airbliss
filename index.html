Current Situation of Delhi

New Delhi ties for first place, along with Beijing, China, for having the world’s worst air. New Delhi is one of India’s most important cities considering the fact that it is India’s capital. Unfortunately for its inhabitants, New Delhi’s air is fatal. According to estimates, air pollution levels after diwali hit 999 micrograms of particulate matter per cubic meter in some areas of the national capital, more than 15-16 times the limit considered safe.

The numbers of vehicles on the road have increased by sixty-five percent since 2003(According to the searched statistics).The more vehicles on the road mean that there are more harmful pollutants being put into the atmosphere. Another main cause of Delhi’s air pollution is simply all the dirt that is being left in the atmosphere creating a haze throughout the whole city. Geography also plays a role in New Delhi’s traumatic air pollution because it is a landlocked city unlike Mumbai or Chennai. There is not enough recognition for India’s capital, as it will emerge into first place for having the world’s worst air. The air needs to be tamed and it needs to be done now.

Air Quality Indoors

Indoor air quality refers to the air quality within and around buildings and structures, especially as it relates to the health and comfort of building occupants. We generally think that air pollution only affect the outdoor air quality. But, we never pay heed to the sources which degrades the indoor air quality.

Indoor air is 4 to 5 times more polluted than air outside. Average indoor air pollution in India is 375 grams/cubic meter of air while WHO norms do not recommend more than 20 grams/cubic meter.

With people spending up to 70 - 90 percent of their time indoors, long-term exposure to indoor air pollution can cause serious health problems.

In offices, homes, and other indoor areas, poor air quality can both cause various health problems and make existing health problem worse. Currently various agencies have ranked indoor pollution as one of the top threats to public health.

Typical symptoms associated with poor indoor air quality include eye, nose, and throat irritation, headache, nausea, dizziness, and fatigue. In some cases exposure to indoor air pollution can lead to acute and chronic respiratory illnesses including asthma, lung cancer, pneumonia, systemic hypertension, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), Legionnaires’ disease, and humidifier fever.

Nowadays, this problem is growing so much that people have to face its consequences like respiratory problems and heart problems. To make our homes and offices a better place we have to remove such harmful sources of pollution.

For Homes

Now are homes are also not protected from pollution as indoor air quality is also very bad which can also cause harmful health problems. With many homes having tighter constructions makes it easier for indoor pollutants to accumulate to dangerous levels. Gases from furniture, celling, wall paints, carpets and other home appliances can cause serious health problems to your loved ones in the home. According to World Health Organization, over 4 million people die prematurely from illness attributable to the household air pollution from cooking with solid fuels. Symptoms such as eye, nose and throat irritation, headache, nausea, dizziness and fatigue is frequently noticed in people living in houses with high levels of indoor pollution.

Respiratory system is the most sensitive system. Children can get easily affected by the indoor air pollution as their lungs are very sensitive. As per the WHO observation, more than 50 percentage of premature deaths due to pneumonia among children under 5 are caused by the particulate matter (soot) inhaled from household air pollution.

For Offices

Air quality in our offices may also be poisonous. Indoor air pollution in offices affects the employees and employers as well. Gases from furniture, desktop, laptops and printers, carpeting, false celling, wall paints and even inadequate or improper HVAC design, are some of the primary factors which causes indoor air pollution and it can harm the whole staff. Typical symptoms associated with poor indoor air quality include eye, nose, and throat irritation, headache, nausea, dizziness and fatigue. In some cases exposure to indoor air pollution can lead to acute and chronic respiratory illnesses including asthma, lung cancer, pneumonia, systemic hypertension, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), Legionnaires’ disease, and humidifier fever.
